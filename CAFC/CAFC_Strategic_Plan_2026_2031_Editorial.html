A plan, built together.

An open letter from Chief Kenrick Hackett to the members and partners of the Caribbean Association of Fire Chiefs.

By Chief Kenrick Hackett · 22 May 2026

This strategic plan is yours. It was built across three working days at the 2026 Annual Conference in the Dominican Republic by over 56 stakeholders — including conference sponsors and vendors, senior chief officers, junior officers, and Emergency Medical Services officers from member islands and territories collectively representing Fire suppression, Emergency Medical Services, and integrated Emergency Services agencies. The plan you hold reflects what those stakeholders told us they need from CAFC over the next five years, and what they have committed CAFC to deliver.

Several things distinguish this plan from prior efforts. It begins with feedback collected from members before the conference — not with a draft circulated by the executive. It was authored, debated, and adopted by the stakeholders themselves, with the facilitation team holding only the discipline of the process. Every component is anchored in evidence: chief-officer feedback, the workshop SWOT, the issues-and-gaps consensus, and the goal statements submitted by stakeholders across the membership.

This plan does not gather dust. It is the working agenda of the association until 2031.
Chief Kenrick Hackett · President, CAFC

Quarterly reporting is built into the framework. Annual review at the conference is built into the framework. A mid-cycle review at Year 3 is built into the framework.

To the chiefs who completed the pre-conference feedback instrument, to those who carried the work across three days in Playa Juan Dolio, and to those who now carry the work back to their islands and into the implementation period — thank you. Article I · Who We Are

About CAFC.

The Caribbean Association of Fire Chiefs (CAFC) is the regional professional association for chief officers leading Fire, Emergency Medical Services, and integrated Emergency Services agencies across the Caribbean. The association exists to advance the standards, capabilities, and professional development of its member chiefs and the agencies they lead — and to provide a unified regional voice for Fire and Emergency Services across the islands and territories it serves.

CAFC member agencies span the full range of disciplines that protect Caribbean communities from Fire, medical emergencies, hazardous incidents, and disasters: Fire suppression and prevention, Emergency Medical Services, integrated Fire and Emergency Services, and search and rescue. Member chiefs lead agencies of varying sizes and structures, serving populations from small territorial communities to large national jurisdictions — across a region united by shared geography, shared risks, and a shared commitment to professional excellence in service of the public.

Article II · Method

How this plan was built.

The Caribbean Chiefs-Driven Strategic Plan methodology, developed for CAFC by Global Emergency Services Consulting Group.

This strategic plan was developed using the Caribbean Chiefs-Driven Strategic Plan — a planning methodology designed for CAFC by Global Emergency Services Consulting Group. The methodology recognizes that CAFC's foundational stakeholder voice is not a single jurisdiction's external community, but the chief officers, stakeholders, and partners of each member island and territory.

Phase 1

Feedback Collection

Every chief officer was invited to complete the Chief-Officer Feedback Instrument capturing expectations, concerns, recognized strengths, and community signals.

Phase 2

Feedback Processing

Responses processed anonymously; ranked sections weighted and verbatim sections themed by topic and audience.

Phase 3

Planning Conference

22 senior chiefs and a parallel cohort of 10 junior/EMS officers built the plan across six sessions, May 19–21, 2026.

Phase 4

Publication

Following adoption, the plan was formalized and distributed; implementation began immediately with named owners.

Mission
To strengthen Fire and Emergency Services across the Caribbean by advancing professional standards, advocating for member services and the communities they protect, and promoting best practices in Fire suppression, Emergency Medical Services, Fire protection, search and rescue, and the safeguarding of life, property, and the environment.

This mission speaks directly to the integrated nature of modern Fire and Emergency Services in the Caribbean. CAFC's reach extends beyond Fire suppression alone to include Emergency Medical Services, Fire protection and prevention, and search and rescue. Each discipline is named explicitly because each is essential to the work our member agencies do every day. The standard we hold ourselves to is not minimum compliance, but best practices — identified, advocated for, and advanced across every member agency so that every community we collectively serve is better protected.

Vision
To create a united, resilient Caribbean Emergency Services system that safeguards life, property, and the environment across the region.

When this vision is realized, Fire and Emergency Services chiefs across the Caribbean will work as a unified regional community rather than as separate national or territorial actors. Standards and professional practices will be aligned, with the highest expectations consistently met across member agencies regardless of jurisdiction size or resource base. Every life touched by a CAFC member agency — whether through Fire, medical emergency, search and rescue, or disaster response — will be served with the same standard of professional excellence. The vision is aspirational by design. Five years from now we will not have closed every gap, but we will be measurably further along the path.

Article IV · Honest Assessment

Where we stand.

An honest, two-team SWOT consensus, built at four wall stations on the first day of the conference.

Strengths

Established regional lobbying access to member governments, donors, and stakeholders.

Demonstrated convening authority — 22 senior leaders across 12 islands engaged directly.

Cross-discipline scope covering Fire, EMS, integrated services, and regional coordination.

Existing institutional relationships with regional training providers ready for operationalization.

Credible regional source of research and education on emerging hazards.

Organizational legitimacy — every workshop issue rated within CAFC's sphere of meaningful influence.

Weaknesses

No full-time Secretariat staff, leaving regional chiefs to carry the administrative load.

Heavy dependence on voluntarism for member participation.

Incomplete member representation and lack of formal government ratification in some states.

Member retention vulnerability — risk of country disbandment.

Documented lack of action between conferences.

Absence of an accredited regional training architecture and dedicated EMS pathway.

No regional procurement database; no communications interoperability standard.

Opportunities

Technology-enabled participation models for financially constrained member states.

Existing regional training institutions ready to be operationalized rather than built.

Government and donor appetite for organized regional advocacy.

Growing international attention to climate resilience and disaster recovery investment.

Available pathways for ministerial-level CAFC ratification in member states.

Vendor markets responsive to consolidated regional demand.

International accreditation frameworks (NFPA, NREMT, ICAO 9137, OTAR) ready for adoption.

Threats

Climate change and increasing frequency of natural disasters on member services.

Persistent financial constraints across small-island economies.

Aging equipment and infrastructure without national capital replacement budgets.

Manpower shortages driven by regional labor market dynamics outside CAFC control.

Evolving emergency threats outpacing doctrine, equipment, and training.

Risk of further country disbandment if value-of-membership is not visibly demonstrated.

Continued government inaction on formal CAFC membership ratification.

Article V · The Sixteen

Strategic issues & gaps.

Sixteen issues, in five thematic clusters. Every one passed the Influence Filter — within CAFC's sphere of meaningful influence.

I.Resource & Funding Pressures
1.
Persistent national budget constraints across member states.
Small-island economies face structural fiscal limits that suppress national investment in Fire and EMS.
2.
Aging equipment and infrastructure without capital replacement budgets.
Member services operate beyond design life with no funded replacement plan.
3.
Disaster recovery costs competing with operational budgets.
Hurricane and flood recovery expenditures crowd out routine Fire and EMS spending.
II.Training & Capability Gaps
4.
Absence of an accredited regional training architecture.
No CAFC-recognized accreditation pathway exists for Caribbean Fire and EMS training programs.
5.
Unmet need for a regional training hub and dedicated EMS pathway.
Existing institutions are underutilized; no consolidated EMT-level certification pathway.
III.Governance, Membership & Continuity
6.
No full-time Secretariat to drive action between conferences.
The documented cause of slow project completion.
7.
Heavy dependence on voluntarism limiting member engagement.
Skews decisions toward better-resourced jurisdictions.
8.
Incomplete member representation and lack of formal ratification.
Membership held by individual chiefs rather than sovereign governments.
9.
Member retention vulnerability and risk of further country exit.
CAFC has experienced country disengagement and faces continuing risk if value of membership is not demonstrated.
10.
Documented lack of action between conferences.
The bottleneck flag — action items lose momentum within weeks of conference close.
IV.Operational Interoperability
11.
No regional standard for communication equipment interoperability.
Incompatible radio platforms limiting joint operational capability.
12.
No regional procurement database to consolidate buying power.
PPE, fleet, and equipment purchased agency by agency.
13.
Insufficient documentation infrastructure for government sign-off and funding.
Standing cannot be converted into formal government action without the supporting materials.
V.Climate & Emerging Threats
14.
Climate change and rising disaster frequency outpacing capability.
Hurricane intensity, flooding, wildland Fire, and complex disasters increasing in frequency and severity.
15.
Evolving emergency threats outpacing operational doctrine.
Electric vehicle fires, lithium-ion incidents, and other emerging hazards.
16.
Workforce supply outside CAFC direct control.
Defines the boundary of where association action can move the needle.
01
Pillar I · Training Standardization

Establish CAFC as the Regional Accrediting Authority for Fire and EMS Training.

By December 2031, CAFC will function as the recognized regional accrediting authority for Fire, EMS, and integrated Emergency Services training delivered across the Caribbean — with a published accreditation standard, an active program of training-provider accreditation, and member-state recognition of CAFC-accredited credentials.
Scope · Fire suppression · EMS · Integrated Emergency Services
Why this matters. Addresses Cluster II directly. Nine of the eighteen Component 5B goal submissions named training and credentialing as the highest-priority area — the largest convergence in the data.
Six objectives & critical tasks
1.1 Publish the CAFC Regional Training Accreditation Standard by December 2027.
  • Constitute a Training Standardization Working Group with at least 8 member states.
  • Draft the standard: governance & ethics, curriculum & competency, instructor & facility.
  • Conduct two public-comment rounds before adoption.
Q3 2026 – Q4 2027
1.2 Accredit at least four regional training providers by December 2028.
  • Develop application package and fee structure.
  • Recruit and train a regional assessor cadre of 8 senior chief officers.
  • Conduct site visits and credentialing decisions.
Q1 2028 – Q4 2028
1.3 Achieve 85% of active-duty Caribbean Fire personnel holding FREC or equivalent EMT certification by December 2027.
  • Confirm current certification numbers across member services.
  • Coordinate regional cohort delivery through accredited providers.
  • Publish quarterly progress.
Q3 2026 – Q4 2027
1.4 Establish a regional instructor development pathway by June 2028 — at least 40 CAFC-credentialed instructors.
  • Define instructor competency tiers.
  • Develop a candidate pipeline.
  • Deliver three regional instructor academies.
Q1 2027 – Q2 2028
1.5 Embed mental-health, climate-resilience, and emerging-threat modules into the curriculum by June 2029.
  • Coordinate with Goal 5 on K10/WHO-5 wellness modules.
  • Coordinate with Goal 6 on hurricane, lithium-ion, and EV Fire modules.
  • Conduct provider re-accreditation against the updated standard.
Q3 2028 – Q2 2029
1.6 Achieve formal recognition of CAFC-accredited credentials by at least seven member-state governments by December 2030.
  • Prepare country-by-country recognition packets.
  • Coordinate with Goal 4 on diplomatic outreach.
  • Track recognition status quarterly.
Q1 2029 – Q4 2030

Pillar III · Organizational Governance

Strengthen Governance, Continuity, and Government Recognition.

By December 2030, CAFC will operate under a documented governance framework with a sustainable Secretariat function, an active program of work between annual conferences, and formal recognition of CAFC membership by at least eight member-state governments.
Scope · Cross-discipline coordination at the association level
Why this matters. Addresses Cluster III in full. The plenary flag — lack of action between conferences — makes governance the precondition for every other initiative.
Eight objectives & critical tasks
2.1 Adopt a documented CAFC Governance Framework by December 2026 (Haughton target).
  • Constitute a Governance Working Group.
  • Draft, circulate, and adopt through a special membership vote.
  • Publish to the membership and website.
Q3 2026 – Q4 2026
2.2 Stand up a sustainable Secretariat function by June 2027 with three years of committed funding.
  • Define Secretariat scope: administration, member relations, project tracking, communications.
  • Identify funding sources blending member dues, sponsor revenue, and donor support.
  • Recruit and onboard initial Secretariat staff or contracted support.
Q1 2027 – Q2 2027
2.3 Stand up five regional technical committees by June 2027.
  • Recruit committee chairs.
  • Publish each committee's charter and 2027 work plan.
  • Establish reporting cadence to CAFC leadership.
Q3 2026 – Q2 2027
2.4 Achieve a 30% increase in active membership participation by December 2028.
  • Baseline participation rates.
  • Publish member value-proposition materials.
  • Implement technology-enabled participation pathways.
Q1 2027 – Q4 2028
2.5 Achieve formal ratification by at least eight member-state governments by December 2030.
  • Develop a ministerial-level ratification packet for each state.
  • Conduct in-country engagement led by the senior chief.
  • Track and publish ratification status quarterly.
Q1 2027 – Q4 2030
2.6 Implement the CAFC government-recognized membership-fee model (Richardson target) by June 2029.
  • Benchmark against the CDEMA membership architecture.
  • Negotiate fee schedules with at least six member states.
  • Operationalize collection through the Secretariat.
Q3 2028 – Q2 2029
2.7 Publish a quarterly CAFC Implementation Progress Report beginning Q4 2026.
  • Build the live issues tracker as the Secretariat reporting backbone.
  • Standardize the quarterly report template.
  • Distribute to the membership and post on the CAFC website.
Q4 2026 onward
2.8 Conduct a mid-cycle plan review at the 2029 Annual Conference.
  • Audit each goal against its milestones.
  • Conduct a feedback survey.
  • Publish the mid-cycle review report.
Q1 2029 – Q3 2029

Pillar II · Mutual Aid & Sourcing

Build a Regional Mutual Aid and Interoperability Framework.

By December 2030, CAFC will publish and operationalize a regional mutual-aid framework, adopt a communications interoperability standard, and stand up a regional procurement database accessible to all member services.
Scope · Fire suppression · EMS · Integrated Emergency Services
Why this matters. Addresses Cluster IV in full. Climate threats cannot be answered without regional mutual aid in place.
Six objectives & critical tasks
3.1 Publish the CAFC Regional Mutual Aid Framework by December 2027, with signed MOUs from at least eight member services.
  • Draft the framework covering activation, command, logistics, cost recovery.
  • Conduct two regional consultation rounds with CDEMA.
  • Execute MOUs with participating services.
Q3 2026 – Q4 2027
3.2 Adopt a Regional Communications Interoperability Standard by June 2028.
  • Inventory current platforms across member services.
  • Draft the standard with regional vendor input.
  • Adopt through CAFC leadership and publish.
Q1 2027 – Q2 2028
3.3 Stand up a Regional Procurement Database by December 2027.
  • Survey member services on procurement volumes.
  • Develop database structure and data-sharing protocols.
  • Execute pilot consolidated purchasing for one category.
Q3 2026 – Q4 2027
3.4 Execute at least three CAFC-coordinated consolidated procurement rounds by December 2030.
  • Identify target equipment categories.
  • Aggregate member-service demand and conduct vendor negotiations.
  • Track and publish cost-savings outcomes.
Q1 2028 – Q4 2030
3.5 Conduct at least one regional mutual-aid exercise per year beginning 2027.
  • Plan annual exercise scenarios.
  • Coordinate logistics with host service and CDEMA.
  • Publish after-action reports.
Annual, 2027 onward
3.6 Integrate the mutual-aid framework with CDEMA regional disaster mechanisms by December 2028.
  • Conduct joint CAFC-CDEMA technical sessions.
  • Execute MOU with CDEMA.
  • Conduct one joint CAFC-CDEMA exercise.
Q1 2027 – Q4 2028

Pillar V · Workforce Wellness

Protect the Mental Health and Wellness of the Regional Emergency Services Workforce.

By December 2030, CAFC will operate a Caribbean Critical Incident Stress Management (CISM) protocol, will have completed a regional K10 and WHO-5 baseline assessment, and will have integrated mental health and wellness standards into the regional accreditation architecture under Goal 1.
Scope · Fire · EMS · Integrated, with particular attention to first responders
Why this matters. Anchored in the Smith, Akogun, Byer, and Warren (2025) CARICOM cross-sectional study documenting elevated psychological distress and reduced wellbeing among Caribbean Fire and EMS personnel.
Nine objectives & critical tasks
5.1 Complete a regional K10 and WHO-5 baseline assessment by December 2027.
  • Define the sampling frame across 8+ services.
  • Execute with appropriate ethics review.
  • Publish baseline findings.
Q3 2026 – Q4 2027
5.2 Publish the Caribbean CISM Protocol by June 2028.
  • Adapt Mitchell & Everly CISM framework to the Caribbean context.
  • Consult with ICISF, regional clinicians, and chief officers.
  • Adopt and publish to the membership.
Q1 2027 – Q2 2028
5.3 Train and deploy regional CISM peer-support teams in 8+ services by December 2029.
  • Define team composition, training, activation protocols.
  • Deliver regional peer-support training cohorts annually.
  • Track team activations and outcomes.
Q3 2027 – Q4 2029
5.4 Establish a regional CISM activation pathway by June 2028.
  • Define activation triggers across incident types.
  • Stand up a 24-hour activation channel.
  • Document after-action care expectations and clinical escalation.
Q3 2027 – Q2 2028
5.5 Integrate mental health and wellness modules into the CAFC accreditation curriculum (Goal 1) by June 2029.
  • Coordinate with Goal 1 lead on curriculum integration.
  • Develop instructor materials and assessment criteria.
  • Roll modules into the provider re-accreditation cycle.
Q3 2028 – Q2 2029
5.6 Secure clinical escalation pathways in 8+ services by December 2030, with CARPHA.
  • Engage CARPHA and ministry-of-health counterparts.
  • Document pathway protocols specific to first responders.
  • Track utilization while protecting confidentiality.
Q1 2028 – Q4 2030
5.7 Conduct a follow-up K10 and WHO-5 assessment by December 2030.
  • Replicate the baseline sampling frame.
  • Execute the follow-up assessment.
  • Publish findings to inform the next planning cycle.
Q1 2030 – Q4 2030
5.8 Publish family-wellness and suicide-prevention guidance by December 2028.
  • Develop materials for responder families and command staff.
  • Conduct member-service consultation rounds.
  • Publish and distribute through the Secretariat.
Q3 2027 – Q4 2028
5.9 Establish a regional mental-health research agenda by June 2029.
  • Define priority questions emerging from the baseline.
  • Engage a research partner through formal procurement.
  • Publish the agenda and seek donor support through Goal 4.
Q3 2028 – Q2 2029

Pillar VI · Climate Resilience

Embed Climate Resilience and Emerging-Threat Preparedness in Regional Doctrine.

By December 2030, CAFC will publish regional doctrine, training modules, and equipment guidance for climate-driven incidents and emerging hazards — delivered through the accreditation architecture under Goal 1 and the operational framework under Goal 3.
Scope · Fire · EMS · Integrated · Cross-discipline
Why this matters. All Component 3 stakeholder respondents converged on climate as a strategic priority, and both Component 4 teams independently identified climate change as the dominant external threat.
Five objectives & critical tasks
6.1 Publish the CAFC Regional Hurricane Response Doctrine by December 2027.
  • Synthesize after-action lessons from Hurricane Melissa and prior events.
  • Conduct regional consultation rounds.
  • Adopt and publish through the Goal 1 accreditation pathway.
Q3 2026 – Q4 2027
6.2 Publish regional doctrine and training modules on lithium-ion and EV Fire response by December 2028.
  • Synthesize current international guidance for the Caribbean context.
  • Develop training modules and instructor materials.
  • Roll into the Goal 1 curriculum.
Q1 2027 – Q4 2028
6.3 Publish the CAFC Wildland-Urban Interface (WUI) Response Framework by December 2029.
  • Inventory WUI risk profiles across member services.
  • Develop framework covering prevention, response, and recovery.
  • Roll into Goal 1 curriculum and Goal 3 operations.
Q1 2028 – Q4 2029
6.4 Establish climate-resilience equipment guidance for four major categories by December 2029.
  • Survey current equipment vulnerabilities.
  • Publish equipment standards reflecting Caribbean conditions.
  • Roll into the Goal 3 procurement framework.
Q3 2027 – Q4 2029
6.5 Coordinate annual climate-resilience tabletops with CDEMA beginning 2027.
  • Plan annual scenarios.
  • Coordinate with Goal 3 mutual-aid exercise calendar.
  • Publish after-action reports and roll lessons into doctrine.
Annual, 2027 onward
